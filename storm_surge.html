What is Storm Surge?

Storm surge is the abnormal rise or fall in water level caused by weather patterns (wind, atmospheric pressure). Values shown are relative to predicted astronomical tide. Positive values indicate higher water than predicted, negative values indicate lower water than predicted. Total water level = Predicted tide + Storm surge.

Model Information

About GDSPS

The Global Deterministic Storm Surge Prediction System (GDSPS) is Environment Canada's operational ocean model that forecasts storm surge conditions worldwide. The system generates 10-day (240-hour) predictions of water level changes caused by atmospheric conditions including wind stress and barometric pressure. Running on a high-resolution grid (approximately 3-9 km spacing), the model integrates atmospheric forcing data with ocean dynamics to predict how weather patterns will affect coastal water levels.

The model separates storm surge (weather-driven water level changes) from astronomical tides through harmonic analysis, allowing forecasters and coastal communities to see how much higher or lower water will be compared to normal tide predictions. This separation is crucial for coastal flood planning and marine safety.

⚠️ Important Note:

Storm surge forecasts do not include wave action (wave setup, wave runup, or breaking waves). Actual water levels during storms may be higher than predicted surge values due to wave effects.

Technical Details

Provider: Environment and Climate Change Canada

Resolution: 1/12° (3-9 km)

Update Frequency: Twice daily (00Z and 12Z model runs)

Data Availability: Forecasts typically published ~6 hours after model initialization

Forecast Horizon: 10 days (240 hours)
